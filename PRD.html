Automated Portfolio Monitoring System

Product Requirements Document — Dual-Engine Architecture · Portfolio Monitoring

Overview
What we are building
Star Mountain Capital is building an automated system to monitor the financial health of its lower-middle-market portfolio companies and flag those at risk as early as possible. This document specifies that system as two forecasting engines that run side by side within one shared pipeline.
Approach
This PRD documents two forecasting engines that run concurrently within one monitoring system. Both are strong, principled designs that reason about the problem differently. We build both, measure where each performs best through backtesting and live use, and let real data guide which elements survive and whether they eventually combine.

The goal is early risk flagging, not prediction. This reframing is the foundation for nearly every decision that follows, so it belongs first. With only a few years of data per company and an ocean of unobserved macroeconomic and cyclical forces acting outside that data, precisely predicting next period's revenue, COGS, or EBITDA is not achievable — and, crucially, it is not the objective. The objective is to detect, as early as possible, when a company deviates meaningfully from its own expected behavior, and to surface that company to the team while there is still time to act. You do not need to predict the future to notice a departure from normal; you need a model of what normal looks like for each company and a disciplined way to notice when reality pulls away from it. That is closer to anomaly detection than to forecasting, and — unlike precise prediction — it is a bar the available data can actually clear.

Why two engines. The best system is not knowable from the whiteboard. It is an empirical question that only backtesting and live performance can answer, because machine-learning outcomes are driven at least as much by the data as by the architecture. Committing to a single design up front is therefore premature. This is worth stating plainly, because it is the same intellectual move made twice at different scales: the Deterministic Engine already runs many candidate models in parallel for each target and lets performance prune them — precisely because it cannot see the best model in advance. Running two whole engines is that identical logic one level up. The four-way model bake-off within an engine and the two-engine system around them are the same idea — start wide, let evidence narrow — applied at two scales. Framed this way, the whole system is a structured exploration that converges through evidence rather than a fixed cathedral poured in one go. It also reframes the "arbitrary decision registers" documented in each engine tab: they are not a list of guesses to apologize for, they are an explicit research agenda — the enumerated set of questions backtesting exists to resolve.

How the two engines relate. The engines are far more alike than different. They share the same data pipeline, the same finance spine (which line items are modeled versus derived by accounting identity), the same covenant framework, the same monitoring quadrant, and the same dashboard. They differ in exactly one stage — the forecasting core. One produces point estimates; the other produces full probability distributions. Everything upstream of that fork and everything downstream of it is shared. This document is organized to make that shape obvious: shared tabs are stated once and apply to both engines, and each engine tab describes only its own forecasting core.

Relationship between the two engines. The Deterministic Engine is the established design — the ensemble of regressions and classifiers producing point estimates — and it is preserved here in full, faithfully and as an equal. The Probabilistic Engine is introduced alongside it, together with the shared architecture that lets both engines feed a single rating system. The two are documented within one coherent system, and this PRD is explicit throughout about where they are identical, where they fork, and how their outputs relate. The Tech Spec and Roadmap tabs are retained from the existing PRD unchanged.

Integration, down the line. "Integrating the two engines" can mean two quite different things, and it is worth separating them — both are forward-looking and explicitly conjecture pending what backtesting reveals. The first is an ensemble at the output: because the convergence architecture already forces both engines to express themselves as the same two risk axes, their outputs can simply be blended — a company flagged by both ranks higher than one flagged by either alone. This is clean and near-term achievable precisely because both engines already exit into the same two numbers. The second is to fuse the internals: for example, using the Deterministic Engine's rich learned feature relationships inside the Probabilistic Engine's state structure, or feeding one engine's output as an input to the other. This is deeper and potentially more powerful, but it is a genuine research project, not a configuration change — point estimates and full distributions do not trivially merge, which is exactly why internal fusion is a real open question rather than a switch we can flip. We state the output ensemble as a realistic near-term option and internal fusion as aspirational and contingent on evidence.

System architecture
The hourglass
The whole system has the shape of an hourglass: shared inputs narrow down to a single finance spine, fork into the two engines at the one stage where point estimates and distributions are genuinely incompatible, then reconverge and share everything downstream. This diagram is the spine of the entire document — every later tab is a zoom into one of its bands.
Diagram 1 — The hourglass. Color encodes node role, identically on both engines; the engines are separated by position, not color.

Read the diagram top to bottom. The shared top narrows: raw financials are cleaned and templated, features are constructed, and everything funnels into a single finance spine that fixes which line items are genuinely modeled and which are derived by accounting identity. Up to this point there is only one pipeline, and it is identical for both engines.

At the finance spine the pipeline forks — and it forks at exactly one stage, the forecasting core, because that is the only place where the two engines are genuinely incompatible. One core treats each driver as a single number and carries points through the identities; the other treats each driver as a random variable and carries distributions. Everything else they could share, they do share.

Below the fork the pipeline reconverges. Both engines exit into the same covenant projection and the same risk positioning stage, which reconciles their outputs onto the firm's rating scale and places each company on the monitoring quadrant, and both present through the same dashboard. The one subtlety to hold onto: although the fork is a single stage, its consequences ripple downstream. Because one engine hands over points and the other hands over distributions, covenant projection and risk positioning must each accept two kinds of input — a point path or a full distribution — even though the framework around them is shared. That is why those two stages sit at the neck of the hourglass rather than cleanly on one side of the fork.

Finance spine · shared
Modeled drivers vs. derived lines
This tab is stated once and shared by both engines. It grounds the whole system in the actual accounting: both engines model the same handful of genuine drivers and derive every downstream line by the same identities. Only the representation differs — a point in one engine, a distribution in the other.

The governing principle is simple: model the genuine drivers, and derive everything that is an accounting identity. A line that is defined by arithmetic — gross profit, EBITDA, free cash flow — must never be independently forecast. If it were, the statements would stop tying, and you would be injecting model error into a place where arithmetic should reign. Deriving these lines instead of forecasting them guarantees two things that matter enormously for a credit tool: internal consistency (the statements always tie, every period, by construction) and interpretability (every covenant flag traces back to a specific driver that moved, not to an opaque blend of forecasts).

The five modeled drivers are Revenue, Gross Margin %, OpEx, Working-Capital turnover (DSO / DIO / DPO), and Capex. Everything else follows from them by identity:

COGS = Revenue × (1 − Gross Margin %)
Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS
Reported EBITDA = Gross Profit − OpEx
Adjusted EBITDA = Reported EBITDA + Addbacks
Working-Capital Change = Δ(turnover-implied working capital)
Interest Expense = debt balance × applicable rate (from each PortCo's credit-agreement terms)
Free Cash Flow = Adj. EBITDA − Capex − ΔWC − Interest − Cash Taxes
Net Debt = prior Net Debt − FCF + revolver draws
Covenant ratios = functions of the above

Why we transform the drivers. We express the drivers in scale-free or transformed terms — but it is worth being precise about the reason, because the obvious one is not the operative one. It is not primarily for cross-company comparability; that motivation dissolves once we decide to pool models rather than raw numbers (see the Probabilistic Engine tab). The operative reason is that each company's own model behaves far better numerically in transformed space, for three concrete reasons. First, financial growth is multiplicative — revenue grows by roughly 3% a month, not by a fixed number of dollars a month — and a linear model wants additive dynamics, so we work in log or growth space where multiplicative growth becomes additive. Second, variance grows with company size — a larger company has larger absolute swings — and log space stabilizes that, so the noise looks roughly constant regardless of scale. Third, log space enforces positivity — revenue cannot go negative, and modeling its logarithm makes that impossible by construction.

So Revenue is modeled as log revenue; Gross Margin as a rate (optionally logit-transformed, since it is a bounded proportion); OpEx as a log or as a ratio to revenue (which captures operating leverage directly); and Capex often as a percentage of revenue. The exact per-line representation is a knob — (provisional — backtest-determined).

Finance spine · shared
Addbacks
Addbacks are where management discretion enters the numbers most aggressively, and Adjusted EBITDA is the covenant denominator — so addback treatment moves every leverage covenant directly. This is not a footnote; it is one of the most consequential lines in the whole system.

What addbacks are. Addbacks are amounts management adds back to reported EBITDA to arrive at "Adjusted EBITDA," on the argument that these costs are not part of ongoing operations — one-time legal or restructuring costs, owner-compensation normalization, non-recurring fees, transaction costs, and sometimes aggressive run-rate adjustments. The credit danger is direct: inflating addbacks manufactures covenant headroom that may not be real. Growing addbacks are a classic credit-quality red flag, because they can quietly convert a deteriorating business into one that still looks compliant.

Classification (shared logic across both engines). An addback that appears in three or more consecutive quarters is classified recurring — a sponsor monitoring fee, for example — and is projected forward as a trailing four-quarter average. Sporadic items are classified non-recurring and are assumed to diminish. Separately, a creep flag fires if any addback's trailing amount exceeds 120% of its historical average; that flag feeds the narrative as a credit signal, separately from the core number. All thresholds are (provisional — backtest-determined).

Non-recurring decay — and where the engines diverge. The Deterministic Engine projects non-recurring addbacks with a fixed exponential decay of 0.75× per quarter (provisional). The Probabilistic Engine instead treats the decay itself as uncertain: the addback contribution decays in expectation but carries a wide, right-skewed distribution — it cannot fall far below zero, but a supposedly "one-time" item can recur upward — and that uncertainty flows into the Adjusted EBITDA distribution and hence into the probability of breach. The reason the Deterministic Engine cannot carry a probabilistic addback is structural, not a matter of effort: its cascade does point arithmetic with no uncertainty propagation, so a distribution introduced at the addback step would be collapsed straight back to a point at the very next identity (Adjusted EBITDA = Reported EBITDA + Addbacks). It has nowhere for the uncertainty to go. Only an engine that propagates distributions end to end can preserve it. So addbacks fork exactly like every other line: shared classification, then deterministic-versus-probabilistic projection.

The honest strength — stated, not oversold. Addbacks are the murkiest and most gameable line in the statements, and they are precisely where "carry the uncertainty" versus "commit to a point" diverges most consequentially, because they sit in the covenant denominator. The Deterministic Engine is architecturally forced to commit to a number there; the Probabilistic Engine can preserve the doubt. Running both is what lets the team see how much that choice actually matters once real data is in — rather than assuming an answer in advance.

Data reality in the lower middle market. In the LMM, addbacks are often reported in aggregate rather than itemized. This spec assumes we can separate recurring from non-recurring items. Where only an aggregate addback time series exists, the fallback is to model the aggregate's level, trend, and persistence as a percentage of EBITDA — which the Probabilistic Engine handles naturally as one more dynamic with its own uncertainty — and to always run the magnitude/growth creep flag, which needs no itemization at all. This is a genuine data-availability dependency and is flagged as such.

Deterministic Engine
How it reasons
This is the established forecasting approach, described faithfully and as one of two genuinely valuable designs. Its strengths are real: interpretable feature-to-target relationships, battle-tested ensemble methods, and a wide-net exploration posture that is exactly right when the best model is not yet known.

The Deterministic Engine learns a mapping from input features to a target value and predicts a single number for each line item. Because it fits a target as a function of many predictors, it captures rich multivariate relationships and stays directly interpretable — you can read which features move a forecast and by how much. And rather than committing to one model per target, it runs several candidate models and selects or prunes them based on measured performance. That is the same wide-then-narrow philosophy as the two-engine system itself, one level down: start with several plausible models, and let evidence decide which survive.

Deterministic Engine
Forecast core — internal nodes
The forecast core forecasts each driver, selects the best model per target, applies the shared addback treatment, and assembles the downstream lines by identity — all as point estimates. A separate credit classifier produces an implied rating.
Diagram 4 — Deterministic forecast core. Everything flows as point estimates; the credit classifier is a separate model producing an implied rating point.

Driver forecasting. Each modeled driver — Revenue, Gross Margin %, OpEx, Working-Capital turnover, Capex — gets its own regression, with a family of candidate models tried per target: Ridge, Lasso, ElasticNet, and a Gradient Boosting Regressor. Each is a genuinely different bet about the shape of the relationship:

Ridge — ordinary least squares with an L2 penalty. It shrinks all coefficients toward zero and handles correlated features gracefully, spreading weight across them rather than picking arbitrarily.

Lasso — least squares with an L1 penalty. It drives some coefficients exactly to zero, performing automatic feature selection and yielding a sparse, readable model.

ElasticNet — a blend of L1 and L2. It selects features like Lasso while handling correlated predictors gracefully like Ridge, which is useful when features are both numerous and collinear.

Gradient Boosting Regressor — a non-linear ensemble of decision trees built sequentially, each tree correcting the residual errors of the trees before it. It captures non-linear interactions the linear models cannot, but it is data-hungry and prone to overfitting on small samples — a real caution at this portfolio size.

Model selection. All candidates are fit per target, their error is measured by cross-validated RMSE, and the best performer is kept. Over time the candidate set is pruned and specialized by company and industry, so the engine narrows toward the models that actually work for each kind of business.

Addback treatment. The shared classification applies — recurring versus non-recurring, a fixed decay for non-recurring items, and the creep flag — exactly as described in the Finance Spine tab. In this engine the decay is a fixed 0.75× per quarter.

Cascade assembly. From the forecasted drivers, the engine derives Gross Profit, then EBITDA, then Adjusted EBITDA, then Free Cash Flow, then Net Debt — all by identity, and all as points. The result is one internally consistent projected set of statements.

Credit classifier. Running alongside the forecast, a separate classification model produces an implied internal rating from the company's current financial ratios. Its details are below.

Deterministic Engine
Credit classifier

The classifier predicts an implied internal rating from current financial ratios, trained on the firm's own history of ratios paired with the analyst-assigned 1–5 rating. Several candidate models are tried, each with a distinct character:

Logistic Regression — a linear baseline that natively outputs class probabilities. Simple, stable, and easy to interpret.

Random Forest — a bagging ensemble that grows many trees on random subsets of rows and features and averages them. It is robust and hard to overfit, because the averaging cancels individual trees' idiosyncrasies.

XGBoost — an optimized gradient-boosting implementation with built-in regularization. Highly accurate, but data-hungry.

LightGBM — another optimized boosting implementation, effectively a near-substitute for XGBoost at this portfolio size.

The winner is chosen by cross-validated accuracy. Interpretability comes from SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), a method from cooperative game theory that fairly attributes a single company's predicted score to each input feature, producing a per-company additive breakdown — for example, "leverage pushed risk up 12, weak coverage +8, healthy cash flow −3." One honest caveat: SHAP explains whatever the model actually learned, so on a model fit to little data it can faithfully explain noise. Explanation quality is capped by model quality.

The credit classifier produces a point — a single implied credit score / rating. That point is handed to the shared Risk Positioning stage; the classifier does not perform the final rating reconciliation itself. See the Risk Positioning & Monitoring tab for how the shared stage consumes it — identically to how it consumes the Probabilistic Engine's credit-status distribution.

Probabilistic Engine
How it reasons
This is the most technically involved tab, and the one that most needs to read for two audiences at once. Every concept below is introduced in plain language first, then in mechanism, then in a diagram — the layers are additive, not alternatives.

The Probabilistic Engine holds a probabilistic belief about each company's true underlying condition, and it updates that belief as each month's data arrives. Its output is not a number but a distribution — honest uncertainty about where the company actually is and where it is heading. That is exactly the currency a risk-flagging tool wants, because the useful question is rarely "what is the single most likely revenue" but "how much of the plausible range now sits in dangerous territory."

Three ideas combine to produce that belief. The first is the shared cascade from the Finance Spine — the same drivers and the same identities, only now carrying distributions. The second is a state-space model updated by the Kalman filter, which supplies the time dimension: how a company's hidden condition evolves month to month and how each new report should shift our belief. The third is a hierarchical prior pooled across companies, which supplies the portfolio dimension: how what we have learned from the whole book informs what we expect of any one company, especially a new one. The next three sections take these in turn.

Probabilistic Engine
State-space models and the Kalman filter

The intuition — separate the truth from the measurement. A company's reported monthly financials are its true underlying condition plus noise: accounting timing, one-off items, the ordinary messiness of books closed under deadline. We do not actually want to model the noisy reported number; we want to infer the clean underlying state that generated it. So the model maintains a probability distribution over that hidden state — a best estimate together with honest uncertainty around it — and revises the distribution every month as new reports arrive.

The mechanism. A state-space model is defined by two equations. The state (transition) equation, state[t] = F · state[t−1] + process noise, says this month's hidden state is a function of last month's plus random "process noise" — genuine change we could not predict. For a level-plus-trend model, F simply encodes "new level = old level + old trend; new trend = old trend," and the process noise is precisely what allows the trend to actually change over time. The observation equation, observation[t] = H · state[t] + measurement noise, says the reported financial is the hidden state passed through a mapping H, plus measurement noise. That measurement-noise term is what lets the model treat a surprising month as partly noise rather than chasing every wiggle in the data.

The two noise terms — and their relative size — control everything. Large measurement noise together with small process noise means the model trusts its own internal trend and smooths over surprises, which suits a stable company. Small measurement noise together with large process noise means the model adapts quickly to each new reading, which suits a volatile or rapidly changing company. These noise parameters are exactly what gets pooled across companies in the hierarchy section — they are the compact description of how a company behaves.

The filter loop — two steps every month. First, predict, before seeing this month's data: project last month's state estimate forward through the transition equation to get this month's prediction, with wider uncertainty, because time has passed and the company could have drifted. This is the one genuine forecast, and it is made before the data arrives. Second, update / correct, after the data arrives: compare the actual to the prediction; the gap is the innovation, or surprise. Nudge the estimate toward the data by an amount set by the Kalman gain — new estimate = prediction + gain × (actual − prediction). The gain is high when we trust the data (low measurement noise) and low when we do not. Crucially, uncertainty shrinks in this step, because we have just gained information. So the uncertainty band breathes: it widens in the predict step and narrows in the update step.

Why this is Bayesian updating. The predict step produces a prior for this month; the incoming data is the likelihood; the update step combines them into a posterior. That is Bayes' rule. The Kalman filter is simply the closed-form solution to Bayesian updating in the special case where everything is linear and Gaussian.

A clarification that is easy to get wrong. The posterior is the model's estimate of the true state of the current period — a denoised read on the present — not a prediction of next period. Prediction is the separate act of pushing the posterior forward through the transition equation. In sequence each month: (1) carry last month's posterior forward to get this month's prior, which is the prediction, frozen before any data; (2) observe the actuals; (3) update to get this month's posterior, the denoised truth of the present. The "prediction versus actual" comparison the dashboard tracks is step-1 output against step-2 observation — the innovation — and that single quantity is both the flagging signal and the calibration metric.

How prediction actually works. Prediction is not a separate regression bolted on. It is the transition step of the very same model: project the posterior state forward with next state = F · current state + process noise — applied step by step to reach the target horizon. Both engines forecast out at least one full quarter (three months), which is the minimum useful horizon for covenant testing and early flagging; the state-space form reaches it by projecting forward month by month and reading off the quarter-end distribution. The uncertainty is projected too, and the process noise widens it at every step, so the forecast is a median trajectory with intervals that fan out — the model honestly saying that the further it projects, the less it knows. That makes it trustworthy only for short horizons, roughly one to two quarters, which is exactly right because the goal is early flagging, not long-range forecasting. Because it projects the current trend forward, it cannot foresee a regime break — a lost customer, an acquisition; those enter only as injected exogenous events, for example from board-deck extraction.

Probabilistic Engine
Pooling models, not numbers — the hierarchical prior

The key insight, stated plainly, because it is the crux of the whole design: we do not pool companies' raw financial numbers. A $20M company's revenue and a $120M company's revenue do not compare, and pooling them would simply drown our already-small dataset in scale differences. Instead we pool the models — the compact set of parameters that describe how each company moves. Two businesses of wildly different size can move in very similar ways, and it is that shared behavior we borrow.

What "a model of how a company moves" is. In the state-space model the transition matrix F is fixed by design — it is just the arithmetic of level and trend. What is actually learned per company is a small set of parameters, call it θ (theta): the process-noise variances (how much the level and trend wander month to month — the company's volatility fingerprint), the observation-noise variance (how noisy its reporting is), and its seasonality parameters. θ is just a handful of scale-free numbers that capture the character of how a company moves. A twitchy company has large process noise; a steady, annuity-like company has small process noise. Two companies of very different size can share nearly the same θ — both growing around 5% with moderate volatility — which is exactly why θ pools cleanly when raw revenue cannot.

A vector state, not a stack of independent lines. The state is a vector that carries the level and trend for all drivers jointly, and θ includes the covariance among them — how they move together. This is the essence of an industry's financial physics: does OpEx flex with revenue (a variable-cost business) or stay flat (a fixed-cost one)? Does margin compress when revenue falls (operating deleverage)? A set of independent per-line models throws all of that away; a multivariate state captures it. We start with a parsimonious covariance — modeling the strongest couple of relationships, Revenue↔OpEx and Revenue↔Working-Capital, rather than a full dense matrix that would overfit on thin data — and expand it as backtesting justifies. (Provisional — backtest-determined which covariances to include.)

Pooling. Collect the θ's across all companies and fit a distribution over them. That distribution — "across our book, volatility clusters around here, with this covariance structure" — is the prior. A new company is assumed to have its θ drawn from that distribution until its own data says otherwise.

Clustering — data-discovered "industries." Rather than hand-assigning SIC codes, we cluster companies in θ-space by their dynamics: group the ones that move alike, using unsupervised learning. Companies with similar behavioral fingerprints form a cluster, and each cluster gets its own tighter prior. This natively handles the objections that a hand-drawn taxonomy raises: fuzzy industry boundaries (a company can sit near a boundary or hold partial membership), companies that genuinely sit between industries (fractional cluster membership), and the long tail — a company with no clear cluster falls back to the whole-portfolio prior with wider uncertainty, which is exactly correct, because we genuinely know less about a company that has no comparables. Clusters are labeled after the math, for interpretability — a human names a cluster "working-capital-heavy cyclicals." That is the semi-supervised flavor: the data draws the boundaries, humans name the regions. On a sparse portfolio, expect few and coarse clusters — perhaps three to six — and let the data decide the count (provisional — backtest-determined). One honest note: a multivariate covariance cannot be estimated from a single company's short history, and this is precisely where pooling re-earns its keep, because the covariance structure is borrowed from the cluster until the company has enough of its own data.

Diagram 6a — Cluster-then-label workflow. Companies are grouped by how they behave, not by industry code; groups are named after the math; each becomes a prior a new company can inherit.

Two levels, two evidences. There are two unknowns, and each has its own evidence — resolving this cleanly matters. At the portfolio level, the evidence is all companies' estimated dynamics (their θ's), and it produces the prior. At the company level, the evidence is that company's own actual financials, which update its model away from the shared prior toward its specific reality. It is a two-story structure: the pooled models form the prior on the upper floor, and each company's own numbers are the evidence on the lower floor. The prior washes out as the company accumulates its own history — the pool is training wheels for a new company, and it is gone by the time that company has a couple of years of its own data.

Leave-one-out — breaking the circularity. A company must not help build the prior it is then judged against, or it would effectively see its own data twice and fool itself into false confidence. The fix is to build each company's cluster prior from the other companies in its cluster — leave-one-out — so that its own data is genuinely fresh evidence against a prior it did not contribute to. This is the very same operation as the held-out-companies evaluation split described in the Risk Positioning tab: the thing that makes the model non-circular is the same thing that makes the test honest. Two caveats stated openly: this two-stage approach — estimate the θ's, then pool them — is an approximation of a fully joint hierarchical model; it is easier to explain and defensible, but with a small portfolio the θ's feeding the pool are themselves noisy. That is another thing backtesting checks: does the pooled prior actually beat a plain fixed prior for new companies? If it does not, we fall back to one coarse portfolio-wide prior.

Probabilistic Engine
Distributional cascade

Once the drivers are forecast as a joint distribution, they are pushed through the same accounting identities from the Finance Spine — this time by Monte Carlo. Draw many joint samples of the five drivers, preserving their modeled correlations; form each derived line and each covenant ratio within every single draw; and the derived quantities inherit correctly propagated uncertainty with correlations preserved. A bad revenue draw and its knock-on margin effect move together in the same sample path, rather than being treated as independent accidents. That is why the resulting probability of breach is honest, instead of assuming — as a naive calculation would — that the covenant's inputs are independent. The draw count is around 10,000 (provisional — precision versus compute). This is something the Deterministic Engine structurally cannot do — points have no correlation structure to sample from — and that is stated not as a criticism but as the natural consequence of choosing to carry distributions in the first place.

Probabilistic Engine
How the model is actually fit
A natural question is whether we "choose a model" the way the Deterministic Engine selects among Ridge, Lasso, and gradient boosting. We do not — and the distinction matters for how the engine is built.

One specified model, not a menu. The Deterministic Engine picks an algorithm from a menu because each candidate is a genuinely different machine and no one knows in advance which fits — hence the bake-off. The Probabilistic Engine works differently: the model is already specified — it is the state-space model. The prior and the posterior are not two models we select between; they are the same model at two moments. The prior is the belief before this month's data (last month's posterior carried forward); the posterior is the belief after. The Kalman filter computes the posterior from the prior and the data mechanically. There is nothing to "select" — which is a point in the engine's favor for diagnosability, because there is one model to reason about rather than a rotating winner.

What is chosen versus estimated. Three things are set, and none is a bake-off. The structure — what sits in the state vector (level, trend, seasonality) and which drivers are modeled jointly with what covariances — is a design decision, made once. The hyperparameters — the process- and observation-noise variances and the covariance structure, collectively the dynamics fingerprint θ — are not chosen from a menu; they are estimated from data. This is fitting, not model selection: given the structure, find the parameter values that best explain the history. The priors on those hyperparameters — how wide, and the cluster structure they draw from — are the one genuine modeling choice, specified in design and tuned by backtest.

The one real engineering choice: the estimation machinery. Where a genuine decision enters is how the fitting is done, and there are two families, used for two different jobs:

Diagram 6b — Estimation machinery. The Kalman filter handles the fast monthly state; MCMC handles the periodic hierarchical fit. Which MCMC framework is a backtest-resolved engineering choice, not a modeling one.

The Kalman filter with maximum-likelihood estimation is the fast, closed-form route — valid because the core model is linear and Gaussian — and it does the per-company monthly state updates in milliseconds. Full Bayesian sampling (MCMC) via a probabilistic programming framework is what the hierarchical part needs done properly: pooling the θ's across companies with full uncertainty, handling fatter-than-Gaussian tails, and producing genuine distributions over the hyperparameters rather than point estimates of them. We expect to use both — the Kalman filter for the fast monthly per-company updates, and a sampling framework for the periodic (quarterly) hierarchical re-estimation across the portfolio. That is not a bake-off; it is two computational tools for two jobs within the one model, and it maps exactly onto the monthly-state / quarterly-hyperparameter split described earlier. Which sampling framework (PyMC, NumPyro on JAX for speed, or Stan) is a genuine engineering decision to settle in the backtest phase, and it appears as such in the technology stack and the decision register.

Probabilistic Engine
How this output feeds the shared rating system

The Probabilistic Engine's credit-status read is a distribution — a probability over rating states and distress, derived from where the forecast distribution sits relative to the distress thresholds. That distribution, or a summary of it, is handed to the shared Risk Positioning stage. Exactly like the Deterministic Engine, the Probabilistic Engine does not perform the final reconciliation itself; the shared stage does, consuming a distribution here just as it consumes a point there. See the Risk Positioning & Monitoring tab.

This register mirrors the Deterministic Engine's, and it makes three honest claims — while explicitly not claiming to be assumption-free. First, several of these knobs are data-set rather than hand-set (the pooled priors, the data-chosen cluster count). Second, they are testable: every one resolves against calibration on held-out data, so "is this value right" has an empirical answer, which the point-estimate analogues mostly lack. Covenant projection
Shared framework
This is the cleanest, most visible payoff of the distributional approach — so it gets full room. The framework itself is shared; only the calculation forks.

Both engines use the identical Covenant Registry and machinery, and that plumbing is entirely engine-agnostic. The registry is a structured, per-PortCo store holding every financial covenant, its formula components, its threshold, and any step-down schedule — thresholds that tighten on a set timetable. At runtime, for each company, the system looks up all of that company's covenants, resolves each formula dynamically against the projected inputs, applies the correct threshold — including any future step-down that is in effect for the projected period — and assesses the result. Only financial covenants are in scope: leverage, coverage / FCCR, interest coverage, senior secured leverage, minimum liquidity, maximum capex, and a minimum EBITDA floor. Non-financial covenants — reporting, operational, affirmative — are out of scope. And only the covenants that actually appear in a given PortCo's agreement are computed. This entire framework is retained from the existing PRD and shared by both engines without modification.

Covenant projection
The forked calculation

The only thing that differs between the engines is what the forecast core hands the covenant module.

Deterministic Engine. It projects the covenant inputs as point estimates, computes the ratio, and compares it to the threshold. The output is headroom — headroom % = (threshold − projected ratio) / threshold — together with a binary pass/fail on that single projected path. There is no notion of likelihood: the projected path either clears the threshold or it does not.

Probabilistic Engine. Because the covenant inputs are distributions, "will it breach" becomes "how much of the distribution sits on the wrong side of the threshold" — the probability of breach. It is worth being precise here, because it is easy to state wrongly: P(breach) is not the height of the density at the threshold; it is the area under the distribution beyond the threshold — the tail probability, the CDF tail. For a maximum-leverage covenant, which breaches when the ratio rises above a cap, it is the mass above the cap; for a minimum-coverage covenant, which breaches when the ratio falls below a floor, it is the mass below the floor. A threshold sitting at the distribution's median implies roughly a 50% breach probability; a threshold far out in the tail implies a few percent. Headroom, in the deterministic world, corresponds to how far into the body of the distribution the threshold sits in the probabilistic world.

A subtlety worth stating — correlation. The covenant ratio is a quotient of two projected quantities — Net Debt over Adjusted EBITDA, say — and both are distributions, and they are correlated, because a bad quarter hits both at once. So P(breach) is the tail of a ratio of two correlated distributions. We compute it by Monte Carlo: draw joint samples of numerator and denominator, preserving their correlation; form the ratio within each draw; and count the fraction of draws that breach. This makes no assumption about the shape of the resulting distribution and handles the correlation correctly. A closed-form approximation does exist, but it is fragile precisely under correlation — so the spec calls for Monte Carlo.

Covenant projection
Why covenant breach probability is the depth axis

Covenant proximity is the most concrete, most precisely defined "bad event" a portfolio company faces, and probability of breach is the Probabilistic Engine's strongest and cleanest output. Those two facts together fix a design decision that carries into the next tab: the depth axis of the monitoring quadrant — how bad is it right now, in absolute terms — is anchored to covenant proximity. For the Probabilistic Engine, depth is the probability of breach, or the probability of sitting within a defined margin of any covenant threshold. For the Deterministic Engine, depth is headroom-based proximity on the point estimate. The decision, stated once and carried forward: the depth axis is covenant proximity.

Risk positioning & monitoring
The convergence — two engine outputs, one rating system
This is where the two engines rejoin. Scoring and rating live here, in the shared stage — lifted out of the individual engines — because positioning is downstream of the fork. The monitoring quadrant lives here too, for the same reason.

The shared Risk Positioning stage owns the rating and the two quadrant axes. Each engine's only job is to hand this stage the same kind of input, computed two different ways. The Deterministic Engine hands over points: a credit-status point from its classifier, and point-based covenant proximity (headroom). The Probabilistic Engine hands over distributions: a credit-status distribution, and a probability of breach.

The shared stage then does the same reconciliation regardless of source: it maps the input onto the firm's 1–5 internal rating scale, compares it to the analyst-assigned rating, and computes the rating gap. Because this reconciliation logic lives in the shared stage and not inside either engine, the two engines stay directly comparable and the rating gap is computed identically for both. The 1–5 internal rating is preserved as the authoritative anchor (retained from the existing PRD); each engine is a structured second opinion; and the gap between the engine-implied rating and the analyst-assigned rating is itself a signal worth surfacing.

Risk positioning & monitoring
The two axes

Depth axis — absolute risk, how bad is it right now. This axis is anchored to covenant proximity, the decision carried over from the Covenant Projection tab. For the Probabilistic Engine, depth is the probability of breach, or the probability of sitting within a defined margin of any threshold. For the Deterministic Engine, depth is headroom-based proximity on the point estimate. High on this axis means close to breach.

Velocity axis — deterioration speed, how fast is it worsening. Here the Probabilistic Engine is distinctly strong. Velocity is the recent, accumulated, recency-weighted, standardized surprise — the innovation from the Kalman loop, actual minus predicted, scaled by its own uncertainty and signed for direction. A run of negative surprises — actuals landing repeatedly below the predicted distribution — is deterioration velocity. And it is self-scaling: a volatile company's ordinary wobble does not trigger it, because that company's wide bands absorb the wobble, while a steady company's small but persistent slide does trigger it. That is better than a fixed threshold precisely because it is calibrated to each company's own predictability. The window is the past four months, recency-weighted (provisional — backtest-determined). The Deterministic Engine derives its velocity from its z-score and drift logic — its analogue — and both engines produce a comparable velocity number, so the quadrant works identically for either.

Risk positioning & monitoring
The monitoring quadrant
Retained conceptually from the existing PRD — it is the strongest framework in the original design and is preserved. Two orthogonal axes, deliberately not collapsed into a single composite, because keeping them separate is what preserves interpretability.
Diagram 9 — The monitoring quadrant. Early Warning is the highest-value cell and the whole reason for keeping the two axes separate.

The four quadrants read as follows. Healthy (low depth, low velocity): low risk and not deteriorating; the model confirms the rating; routine monitoring. Watch (high depth, low velocity): already a known risk, close to covenants, but stable and not accelerating; the rating already reflects it, so cadence is maintained — high absolute risk alone is not an alarm if it is not moving. Critical (high depth, high velocity): close to breach and worsening fast; a genuine emergency warranting out-of-cycle review, a covenant check, and a management call. Early Warning (low depth, high velocity): the highest-value quadrant, and the entire reason for the two-axis design. A company here still looks safe on absolute covenant proximity but is deteriorating fast on its own trend — the damage is happening in the trajectory before it shows in the levels. Traditional one-dimensional ratio review, which sees only depth, is structurally blind to this; catching it early, before it migrates into Critical, is the core value proposition of the system.

A probabilistic enhancement. Because the axes are probabilistic, a company's quadrant placement can itself carry uncertainty — it is not simply "in Early Warning" but, say, "80% likely Early Warning, 20% Healthy," which the dashboard can render as a position with a confidence halo rather than a hard dot. The Deterministic Engine can only place a hard dot. Same quadrant, richer read.

Risk positioning & monitoring
Dashboard, cadence, and the feedback loop
Output is not PDFs, Excel exports, or alert-lists — a deliberate change from the original design. Everything lives in one interface in Opstream: a dashboard that refreshes every month, lets the team toggle back to prior months, and shows how past predictions actually performed against what happened.

Monthly auto-refresh. New monthly data kicks off the pipeline automatically. Both engines are comfortable with this cadence, and it is retained from the original design.

Historical toggle and prediction-versus-actual analytics. These are built from an immutable monthly log. Each month, the system freezes that month's inputs, the prediction or distribution, and the flags raised; later it appends the actuals and an analyst outcome tag. That single log is simultaneously the dashboard's history, the audit trail, and the evaluation set — one artifact serving all three purposes.

Continuous improvement comes in three distinct mechanisms — and they must be kept distinct, because one of them carries a subtle trap.

1. Belief updating (automatic, safe). As history accumulates, the model sharpens its estimate of each company. This is simply the engine doing its job, not "improving" in any risky sense. Monthly.

2. Hyperparameter recalibration (periodic, human-gated, safe). As history grows, the tunable settings — the θ's, the thresholds — are re-estimated and reviewed before going live. Quarterly. A change is accepted only if it improves calibration on the frozen log. That frozen-log calibration is the shared referee that stops the monthly and quarterly loops from amplifying each other into a twitchy, over-reactive model.

3. Learning from flag outcomes (powerful, and the one to be careful with). Here is the trap: a flag changes what happens next. If a flag prompts an intervention and the company then recovers, the flag looks wrong in the data — but it was right, and the intervention is why. Feeding that back naively would train the model to stop flagging exactly the companies where flagging worked. So the automatic signal is the calibration of the forecasts, not the flags — because forecasts are intervention-proof: whether next quarter landed in the predicted range does not depend on whether anyone intervened. Flag outcomes are kept as human-labeled context, reviewed periodically to spot patterns, and they feed deliberate, gated model changes — never an automatic retrain on flag labels. Everything is logged immutably; changes are periodic, reviewed events with a before/after calibration comparison, never silent drift. For a tool whose whole value is investment-committee trust, "the model changed, and here is exactly how and why it is better" beats "it continuously self-improves," which is un-auditable and a liability in a risk tool.

Calibration is the north-star metric. When the model said it was 70% confident, did the thing happen about 70% of the time? That is the clean, intervention-proof measure of success, and it is the primary thing the dashboard surfaces. It is also how the two engines are compared honestly.

Cold-start and low-confidence display. For a company with little history and no good cluster, the model says so — "insufficient history, wide bands, low confidence" — rather than showing a falsely confident dot. This applies to both engines, and it is a deliberate design position, so that honest uncertainty is a feature of the interface rather than an awkward gap in it.

Risk positioning & monitoring
How the two engines are compared

Whoever picks the metric picks the winner, so the scoring discipline is settled up front. The primary metric is flagging performance: did the deviations each engine raised correspond to companies that actually got into trouble, and at what false-alarm rate? The secondary metric is calibration, which is directly meaningful for the Probabilistic Engine's distributions; the Deterministic Engine's points must be converted, or the comparison framed appropriately, for calibration to apply. Point accuracy (RMSE) is tertiary and explicitly not decisive — it is reported, but it is not the judge, because it is the one axis the Probabilistic Engine deliberately trades away in exchange for honest uncertainty. Judging on RMSE would be judging one engine on the very thing it chose not to optimize.

There are two evaluation splits, and they test different things. The held-out time split trains on earlier months and tests on later ones; it measures forward flagging, which is the deployment reality. The held-out companies split fits the hierarchy on a subset of companies and tests the prior on entirely new ones; it measures cold-start and onboarding — which is most of the portfolio — and is the direct test of whether pooling actually helps. That second split is the very same leave-one-out operation that breaks the circularity in the hierarchical prior: the thing that keeps the model honest and the thing that tests it are one and the same.

Track A · Data
The data challenge, in detail

The data track is the genuine risk in this program, and the roadmap treats it honestly rather than optimistically. We do not begin from a clean dataset. What we have and what we must do breaks into distinct lanes, each with its own difficulty and its own uncertain timing.

Structured backfilling. The structured data we hold for the initial group of roughly 50 companies is real but incomplete. Before it can drive either engine reliably, gaps must be backfilled. This is the most tractable lane and starts immediately.

Unstructured reconciliation. We also hold unstructured data for these companies. The good news is that it is validated and professionally structured at source, so the work is not cleaning — it is reconciliation: mapping that data into the structure our existing data uses so the two are consistent and jointly usable. This is ongoing and less predictable than backfilling.

Company expansion. We have similar data on additional companies that will require the same reconciliation process. Coverage therefore ramps over time from the initial ~50 toward an estimated 120–150 companies in total, with an average life of roughly five years each (this varies and is not yet precisely known). The expansion lane is what eventually lets the models take full advantage of all internal data.

A scope reducer worth stating. We do not need full, detailed three-statement models for every company. The system is concerned only with the higher-level line items that drive the forecast and the covenants, which materially reduces the reconciliation burden per company and makes the ~120–150 target realistic.

External data (later, optional). Eventually we may scope out purchasing external data to enrich the models. This is deliberately placed late and marked optional; it is an enhancement, not a dependency, and it is contingent on what the internal data and the engines reveal first.

Track B · Infrastructure & Engines
Building now on what we have

The infrastructure and both engines are built starting now, on the roughly 50 companies for which we already have workable data — we do not wait for the data track to finish. This lets us stand up the pipeline, storage, the immutable monthly log, and the Opstream dashboard early, and get both the Deterministic and Probabilistic engines running against real (if partial) data.

The two engines are developed concurrently, as peers. Running against the initial cohort, the evaluation-and-convergence lane compares them on flagging performance and calibration, and — just as importantly — prunes techniques that do not earn their place. Over the course of the buildout we expect to settle on a clearer path forward: which engine, or which combination, is carrying the weight, and which candidate methods within each have been dropped.

Cross-track milestone
Full data readiness → architecture overhaul

The two tracks converge at a single pivotal milestone: the point at which the full internal dataset has been reconciled and is usable. Based on the data challenges above, we expect this to land at least into 2027 — the roadmap marks it as a milestone rather than a fixed date, because its timing depends on the data work and should not be over-promised.

Reaching that milestone, combined with the technique pruning and convergence achieved on the engine side along the way, sets up an architecture overhaul toward a steady-state production solution. By that point we expect to have a much clearer picture — the exploratory breadth of two engines and multiple candidate methods will have narrowed toward the approach that actually works on our data at full scale. The overhaul is intentionally left open in scope here; it will be defined when we reach it, informed by everything learned in the interim rather than guessed at now.
